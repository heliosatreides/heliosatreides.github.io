A running list of AI startup ideas I find interesting — specific gaps, not generic "AI for X" hand-waving. Some I might build. Some I'm just logging so they exist somewhere.

---

- **Freight Broker Carrier Intelligence CRM** — When a freight broker leaves a brokerage, they take everything with them: which dispatchers to call, preferred lanes per carrier, negotiated rate history, who answers on weekends. None of that lives in any system — it's in people's heads and phones. An AI CRM built specifically for freight brokerages that captures call notes (via transcription), automatically extracts carrier preferences, rate agreements, and relationship signals, and makes that knowledge institutional rather than personal. New brokers ramp faster; turnover stops being catastrophic. Revenue model: per-seat SaaS, $80-150/seat/month targeting 10–200 broker teams. The trucking industry is $800B and still runs on spreadsheets, sticky notes, and tribal knowledge. *(Added 2026-03-24)*

- **SaaS Renewal Negotiation Copilot for SMBs** — Most small businesses pay rack rate for every SaaS tool because they don't have a procurement team and don't know negotiation is even on the table. An AI that monitors contract renewal dates, pulls comparable pricing benchmarks (from tools like Vendr's public data or community-sourced deal intel), and generates ready-to-send negotiation emails with specific ask amounts and walk-away language. Revenue model: 20% of first-year savings. Target: 10–200 person companies spending $50K+/yr on software. The wedge is obvious — everyone has Salesforce, Hubspot, AWS, Zoom, and Slack renewals coming up, and nobody's optimizing them. *(Added 2026-03-24)*

- **Insurance Subrogation Screener** — When insurers pay out a claim, they have a legal right to recover that money from at-fault third parties — this is called subrogation. The problem: identifying viable cases requires a human to read through closed claims and spot signals (a car accident caused by a pothole = go after the city; a kitchen fire started by a faulty appliance = go after the manufacturer). Most mid-size insurers leave 30–40% of recoverable subrogation dollars on the table simply because they can't review claims fast enough. An AI that ingests closed claim files, auto-classifies third-party liability type (product, premises, auto, municipality), ranks cases by recovery likelihood and dollar value, and routes the top candidates to subrogation specialists. Revenue model: $0.50–$2 per claim screened + 5–10% of recovered amounts above baseline. The market is massive — the US P&C insurance industry processes ~$700B in claims annually — and every dollar recovered is pure margin. Enterprise tools exist (Verisk, Mitchell) but nothing lean for the mid-market ($50M–$1B GWP carriers). *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Veterinary Insurance Claims Automation** — Pet insurance is one of the fastest-growing insurance segments ($4B+ in the US, growing 20%+ annually), but vet practices still handle claims the same way they did in 2010: print the invoice, hand it to the client, and hope they submit it correctly. Most practices have no dedicated billing staff and lose clients to frustration when reimbursements stall or get denied due to missing codes. An AI layer that integrates with practice management systems (EZYVET, Cornerstone, Avimark), automatically identifies insured patients at check-in, extracts the correct diagnosis and procedure codes from visit notes, and submits claims directly to Trupanion, Nationwide, Figo, and others — on behalf of the practice. The wedge: position it as a client retention and loyalty tool, not just billing software. Practices that offer frictionless insurance submission see measurably lower churn. Revenue model: $250–500/month per practice or $4–6 per claim submitted. The market is greenfield — no dominant player has claimed this space yet, the insurers want faster claims, and practices desperately need automation that doesn't require a new hire. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **EU AI Act Compliance Copilot** — The EU AI Act's high-risk AI provisions take effect August 2026, and most companies deploying AI systems have no idea how to comply. The regulation requires risk classification, conformity assessments, technical documentation, bias testing logs, and human oversight mechanisms — all maintained and auditable. Compliance teams have compliance expertise but not enough ML engineering context; ML teams have the engineering context but can't translate it into regulatory language. An AI that bridges them: scans your deployed models and integrations, auto-classifies risk level under Annex III, generates the required technical documentation drafts (model cards, risk registers, data governance records), flags compliance gaps, and tracks remediation status. Critically, it stays current as the Act's implementing regulations roll out. Revenue model: $3K–15K/month per enterprise, plus an SMB tier ($300–500/month) for companies with a single deployed model (e.g., a customer service chatbot). Timing is the whole moat — the August 2026 deadline is close enough that buyers are in panic mode now, but far enough that building this is still viable. No established player owns this space yet. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Small Claims Court Prep AI** — Every year, millions of Americans have a legitimate grievance — a landlord who won't return a security deposit, a contractor who took money and vanished, a seller who shipped a broken product — but never pursue it because small claims court feels intimidating and opaque. The process varies by state, filings are confusing, and the person on the other side often has experience or an attorney. An AI that takes someone through the full prep process: upload your evidence (texts, emails, receipts, photos), describe what happened in plain language, and get back a structured case narrative, the correct legal theory (breach of contract vs. negligence vs. consumer protection statute), the right jurisdiction and filing venue, pre-filled court forms for your specific state, a demand letter to send first (which often settles without court), and a mock cross-examination so you're not blindsided. Revenue model: $49–79 one-time per case. Upsell: $149 for a 30-minute async review by a paralegal who checks your filing before you submit. The legal aid space is dominated by business formation (LegalZoom) and expensive litigation (traditional law firms) — the messy middle of everyday consumer disputes is almost entirely unserved. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **IEP Documentation AI for Special Education Teachers** — Special education teachers in the US are legally required to maintain an Individualized Education Program (IEP) for every student with a disability — a detailed, regulation-compliant document covering present performance levels, annual goals, accommodations, services, and progress reports. These must be updated at least annually, sometimes more often, and the consequences of procedural errors are serious (due process complaints, district liability). The average special ed teacher manages 15–25 IEPs simultaneously and reports spending 30–40% of their working time on documentation rather than instruction. They routinely write these after school hours, often using language from prior years with minimal adaptation because the templates are confusing and the stakes are high. An AI that takes a teacher's raw session notes, progress data, and conversational input, then drafts IEP-compliant language — present levels, SMART goals, service minutes — mapped to the correct state standards and pre-formatted for the district's special ed platform (Frontline, IEP Pro, SEIS, Aries). It also flags missing required sections and checks new IEP language against the prior year to catch inconsistencies before the annual meeting. Revenue model: $60–100/month per teacher, or $8K–20K/year per district for a site license covering all special ed staff. There are 500K+ special ed teachers in the US managing 7.5M active IEPs — this is a massive compliance burden with no modern tooling. The existing special ed software (Frontline, etc.) handles storage and workflows but does nothing for the actual writing. That gap is the opportunity. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Construction Draw Lien Waiver Tracker** — On every commercial or multi-family construction project, each progress payment (draw) legally requires collecting conditional and unconditional lien waivers from every subcontractor and supplier on the job — before the owner releases the next payment. Miss one, and any sub can file a mechanic's lien on the property, cloud the title, and stall financing or sale. General contractors manage this entirely manually today: emailing PDFs, chasing DocuSign completions, tracking who's outstanding on a spreadsheet. An AI that integrates with Procore, Buildertrend, or Sage 300, auto-generates state-specific lien waiver forms pre-filled with the correct draw amounts and dates for each sub, routes them for e-signature, tracks real-time completion, and surfaces a "draw is clear to release" dashboard before the GC submits the owner draw request. No strategic value in this workflow — it's pure compliance drag — but the cost of getting it wrong (a lien on a $20M project) is enormous. Revenue model: $600–2,000/month per GC firm based on active project count; target mid-size GCs running 3–20 concurrent projects. The space is shockingly vacant: Procore has a lien waiver module but it's clunky, state coverage is incomplete, and it doesn't handle the collection/reminder loop. Nothing standalone has nailed it. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Auto Body Shop Supplement Negotiator** — When a car gets damaged in an accident, the insurance adjuster writes an initial repair estimate. Then the body shop tears it down and finds additional damage — a bent subframe, hidden hail dents, cracked mounts — and has to negotiate a "supplement" payment from the insurer for the extra work. This back-and-forth is entirely manual: the shop writes up what they found, the adjuster pushes back citing Labor Time Guide references, and shops without experienced estimators routinely leave $500–$5,000 per car on the table or lose the negotiation outright. The problem is asymmetric: adjusters do this 40–50 times a day and know every objection; most independent shop owners handle it a few times a week and have no time to research OEM repair procedures mid-teardown. An AI that ingests the original estimate, the shop's teardown findings, and OEM repair procedure documents (ALLDATA, I-CAR, manufacturer TSBs) and generates a complete supplement submission — with correct repair methodology citations, labor time justifications, and argumentation tailored to that specific insurer's known objection patterns. You train it on that insurer's prior supplement outcomes and it knows where State Farm is soft, where GEICO pushes back, and what language gets Allstate to fold. Revenue model: $12–18/month per repair order submitted, or a 10% share of incremental supplement dollars approved above the original estimate. 35,000+ independent auto body shops in the US handle 25M+ collision claims annually. The vast majority are owner-operated and get systematically outgunned by adjusters who have enterprise tooling on their side. No dedicated supplement-writing AI exists. The estimating platforms (CCC, Mitchell, Audatex) are adjuster tools — they're not built to help shops argue back. That's the gap. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Dental Front Desk Collections Copilot** — Dental offices collect two types of payment: insurance (billed after the visit) and patient out-of-pocket balances. The second bucket — co-pays, deductibles, treatment plan balances — is where most practices quietly bleed. The typical workflow is a front desk coordinator calling a list of overdue patients, leaving generic voicemails, sending paper statements, and hoping. Response rates are terrible, and the coordinator's time is worth more at the front desk scheduling new patients than working a collections list. An AI that handles the outreach end-to-end: texts and emails overdue patients with personalized, HIPAA-compliant messages referencing their specific balance and visit date, offers a one-click payment link tied to the practice's existing payment processor (Stripe, Clover, Transact), suggests a payment plan if the balance is over a threshold, and escalates to human follow-up only when the patient responds with a dispute or question. It also predicts which patients are likely to go delinquent based on payment history and pings the front desk before the visit rather than after. Revenue model: $300–600/month per practice + 1.5% of collections driven through the platform. The dental industry collects ~$160B/year from patients and insurers — the patient-pay portion alone is estimated at $40B+, with 15–20% going uncollected or written off. Existing dental practice software (Dentrix, Eaglesoft, Open Dental) has "collections reports" but no automated outreach loop. That workflow gap is why every dental office still has a staff member working a paper AR list. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Specialty Drug Prior Authorization AI (Provider-Side)** — Getting insurance to cover a specialty drug — a biologic for Crohn's, an MS infusion, an oncology agent — requires submitting a prior authorization (PA) to the payer. 94% of physicians say PA causes care delays; the average practice burns 11+ staff hours per week on them. The core dysfunction: every payer has different approval criteria, different submission portals, different preferred clinical language, and they update everything constantly. Staff navigate this with binders, memory, and a lot of fax machines. The existing tools in the space (CoverMyMeds, PARx Solutions) are designed for payers — they help insurers receive and route PA requests. Nothing is built to advocate for approval on the provider side. An AI that, given the patient's payer, drug, and diagnosis, instantly surfaces exactly what that payer requires for that specific drug (step therapy requirements, lab thresholds, prior treatment history), pre-fills the correct form or drafts a fax-ready submission, generates a clinical justification letter that mirrors the payer's own approval language, and tracks denial deadlines with auto-drafted appeal language if rejected. Over time it learns which payers approve on first submission vs. always require a peer-to-peer, so staff route the hard cases proactively. Revenue model: $1,000–2,500/month per specialty pharmacy; $400–800/month for high-volume oncology, rheumatology, and fertility practices. The market is massive — ~350 specialty pharmacies plus thousands of high-prescribing physician offices process millions of specialty PAs annually, and every denied or delayed PA represents a patient not getting their medication and staff time completely wasted. No one has built the provider-advocacy side of this. That's the gap. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Municipal Permit Intake AI for Small Cities** — Most small and mid-size municipalities (population 5K–100K) still accept building permits, zoning variances, and business license applications via paper forms, email, or a generic PDF upload portal. Applications routinely arrive incomplete — missing a site plan, wrong fee attached, zoning district not specified — which triggers a round of back-and-forth phone calls or mailed rejection letters that stall projects for weeks. The permit technicians fielding these calls have deep local knowledge but spend the majority of their time on intake triage, not review. Enterprise GovTech platforms (Tyler Technologies, OpenGov, Accela) cost $100K–$500K+ and take 18 months to implement — priced entirely out of reach for a town of 15,000. An AI intake layer that embeds into the city's existing website, walks applicants through a plain-language questionnaire to determine permit type and jurisdiction, generates a complete checklist of exactly what's required for that specific permit in that specific municipality, validates the submission before it hits the queue (catches incomplete applications before a human ever sees them), and auto-assigns the intake packet to the right reviewer with extracted key fields pre-filled in the city's existing system. The AI learns the local code over time — which zones require conditional use permits, what the local setback requirements are, where the floodplain boundaries sit. Revenue model: $800–2,500/month per municipality, based on population tier. The total addressable market is 19,500+ cities and townships in the US, the majority of which have zero modern intake tooling. GovTech is notoriously slow to innovate and the bottom of the market is completely ignored — but those small jurisdictions have real budget (they collect permit fees) and real pain (two permit techs managing 400 applications a month by hand). *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Commercial Lease Obligation Tracker** — Companies with 5–200 locations carry a stack of commercial leases that are 50–200 pages each, dense with obligations that can cost a fortune to miss: rent escalation dates, CAM reconciliation deadlines, option exercise windows (often 90–180 days before expiration — miss it and you forfeit the option permanently), co-tenancy triggers, insurance certificate requirements, and TI allowance expiration dates. Big law firms do manual lease abstraction for Fortune 500 companies at $150–300/hour. Mid-market companies manage this in Excel and miss critical dates regularly. An AI that ingests commercial lease PDFs, extracts every obligation and critical date, builds a structured obligation calendar with automated email/Slack reminders ahead of each deadline, projects forward rent costs under escalation formulas, and flags when an option exercise window is opening. The differentiation over a generic document AI: it knows commercial lease law specifically — what "base year stop," "gross-up," and "HVAC exclusion" mean, and it surfaces the tenant's negotiating rights, not just the raw dates. Revenue model: $100/lease for abstraction + $20/month per location for ongoing deadline tracking. Target buyers: retail chains, restaurant groups, medical practices, coworking operators — anyone with a portfolio of locations managed by a two-person ops team. The cost of missing a single renewal option on a prime location is often $200K–$1M in displacement costs. The tooling for this market (CoStar, Accruent, Lucernex) is priced for enterprise. Nothing lean exists for the regional operator who has 20 leases and no VP of Real Estate. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **IDIQ Task Order Pursuit AI for Small GovCons** — Thousands of small and mid-size government contractors hold seats on IDIQ/GWAC vehicles — GSA MAS, CIO-SP3, OASIS, STARS III, SEWP — but capture only a fraction of the task orders flowing through them. The reason isn't lack of capability; it's lack of bandwidth. Task order RFPs drop with 5–10 day windows, arrive fragmented across SAM.gov, agency portals, and eBuy, and each requires a tailored response that references the base vehicle correctly, hits NAICS alignment, maps labor categories to the base CLIN structure, and incorporates relevant past performance. A two-person capture team at a 50-person firm physically cannot track and respond to all of them. An AI that monitors SAM.gov and eBuy continuously for task order opportunities across a firm's specific vehicles, auto-scores each opportunity for fit (scope alignment, set-aside match, agency past performance, competition density), drafts a compliant response shell pre-populated with the correct vehicle and CLIN references, flags which past performance citations are most relevant to that scope, and tracks win/loss patterns by agency and NAICS to improve future scoring. Over time it builds an institutional memory of what language wins at which agencies — something even experienced capture managers don't carry in their heads across the full vehicle portfolio. Revenue model: $2,000–5,000/month per firm, tiered by number of active vehicle seats and monthly opportunity volume. The federal contracting market is $700B+ annually; IDIQ vehicles are how the majority of IT, professional services, and logistics work flows. Most small businesses that fought hard to win a vehicle seat leave 40–60% of their vehicle capacity untapped simply because they can't keep up. No purpose-built tool exists for this pursuit workflow — Deltek GovWin and Govly handle pipeline intelligence but not vehicle-specific response generation. That gap is the product. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Hotel F&B Forecasting AI for Independent Properties** — Food and beverage is the second-largest revenue source for most full-service hotels, and also the most reliably over-staffed or under-prepped operation on property. The forecast problem: a hotel restaurant needs to know, 72 hours out, how many covers to expect for breakfast, whether banquet staff should be called in for Sunday brunch, and whether to order an extra 40 lbs of salmon. Corporate chains solve this with revenue management systems (IDeaS, Duetto) that cost $30K–80K/year and are built for rooms, not F&B. Independent and boutique hotels — roughly 25,000 properties in the US — use gut instinct, last year's numbers, and whoever's been there the longest. An AI that ingests the hotel's PMS reservation data (occupancy, group blocks, arriving nationality mix, rate category), cross-references with historical F&B covers by day-of-week and season, factors in local events (conferences, game days, concerts — via scraping or TicketMaster API), and outputs a 7-day rolling F&B forecast broken down by daypart — breakfast covers, lunch headcount, dinner reservations expected, banquet F&B minimum from contracted events. It also surfaces prep and staffing recommendations: "Friday: add one AM line cook, prep 30 portions of short rib over the board average." The insight that matters most to F&B directors is food cost variance — waste and 86'd items are both revenue leaks — and every percentage point of food cost saved is pure profit. Revenue model: $400–900/month per property. The TAM is tight but the pain is acute and unaddressed: the big RMS platforms won't go downstream, and the F&B vertical is too operationally specific for generic forecasting tools to serve well. Targeting food-forward boutiques and lifestyle hotels (Kimpton-style independents) as the beachhead — they have the volume to care and the margin pressure to pay. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Physician Employment Contract Analyzer** — Every year, 100,000+ physicians sign employment contracts with hospital systems, large practice groups, and DSOs — and most sign without legal representation because healthcare attorneys charge $500–1,000/hour and doctors don't know where to start. The contracts are dense with career-defining terms most physicians only understand after they get burned by them: non-compete radius and duration (a 25-mile radius in a dense metro is very different from a rural county), RVU productivity thresholds and how bonus compensation steps up (or doesn't) as volume grows, call obligations that aren't in the summary, tail coverage liability ($30K–$80K exposure that surfaces the day you leave), and restrictive covenants that block practice in the specialty for years. An AI that ingests a physician employment contract PDF, extracts and explains every material term in plain English, models out total compensation at different productivity levels (at 4,500 RVUs/year vs. 5,500 vs. 6,500 — with the actual wRVU conversion factor applied), benchmarks the comp structure against MGMA survey data for that specialty and region, flags the 5 highest-risk terms for that specific specialty (oncologists have very different leverage points than hospitalists), and surfaces negotiating ask language physicians can bring back to the employer. Upsell: a 45-minute async review by a healthcare attorney who annotates the contract with the AI analysis already done, reducing their time (and the bill to the physician) by 60%. Revenue model: $149–249 per contract analysis; $399 bundled with the attorney review tier. The alternative is paying $1,500–3,000 to an attorney for full review — most physicians skip it entirely and some regret it. Every residency graduating class represents a fresh cohort of first-time contract signers with no frame of reference and real stakes. No purpose-built tool for this exists — DocuSign, Doximity, and the contract review AI tools that do exist are general-purpose. Physician contract language is specialized enough (wRVU, MGMA benchmarks, tail coverage mechanics, CMS anti-kickback implications) that a purpose-built vertical product has durable defensibility over generic contract AI. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Franchise Disclosure Document (FDD) Analyzer** — Buying a franchise is one of the largest financial decisions most people ever make — costs range from $80K for a service-concept territory to $2M+ for a multi-unit restaurant buildout, almost always backed by a personal guarantee. Every franchisor in the US is legally required to provide a Franchise Disclosure Document: a 200-400 page filing with 23 prescribed disclosure items. The document is deliberately designed for protection, not transparency. Item 19 (financial performance representations) is entirely optional — many franchisors omit it, or selectively disclose median gross revenue with zero cost data, making profitability impossible to calculate without guesswork. Item 20 lists every franchisee termination and non-renewal, but prospective buyers almost never know how to interpret churn rate, or what unit velocity (new opens vs. closures per year) signals about system health. Item 3 lists litigation but buries patterns in legalese. Most buyers either pay $2,000–5,000 for a generic attorney review or trust a franchise broker who earns $30,000–60,000 in commission and has zero financial incentive to surface red flags. An AI that ingests the FDD PDF, extracts and benchmarks all 23 items against historical FDDs for that system (from FTC public filings and franchise databases like Franchimp), calculates the net unit growth rate (a system that opened 40 units and closed 38 in 3 years is in distress), flags missing Item 19 disclosures and explains what comparable franchisors typically show, surfaces litigation patterns across Item 3, and generates a due diligence question list calibrated to that specific system's risk profile — the questions to ask during franchisee validation calls that a broker would never give you. Upsell: a 45-minute async attorney review using the AI-generated brief, cutting prep time 70% and the buyer's bill by more than half. Revenue model: $199 per FDD analysis; $449 with the attorney review tier. The market is real: ~8,500 active franchise systems in the US, 15,000–20,000 FDD reviews initiated annually, and the cost of a bad franchise investment is catastrophic. No purpose-built FDD analysis tool exists — attorneys are too fragmented to build it themselves, and no broker has incentive to build something that helps buyers say no. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Skilled Nursing PDPM Documentation Gap Analyzer** — Since CMS switched skilled nursing facilities (SNFs) from RUG-IV to the Patient-Driven Payment Model (PDPM) in 2019, reimbursement is calculated across five case-mix components (PT, OT, SLP, nursing, NTA) based on clinical characteristics coded in the MDS assessment. The problem: the link between a therapist's daily progress note and the MDS codes that determine payment is invisible to most clinicians. An OT who writes "patient requires maximal assistance with transfers" captures something clinically accurate but misses that adding a specific cognitive impairment indicator (under BIMS/GG0130) could shift the patient's nursing component by $80/day — times a 28-day stay, that's $2,240 in uncaptured revenue on a single patient. Simultaneously, over-coding without documentation support triggers RAC audits and repayment demands. An AI that ingests daily therapy notes, nursing assessments, and physician orders, cross-references them against the pending MDS, flags where clinical documentation supports a higher-value code that isn't being captured, identifies coding positions that lack supporting documentation (audit risk), and generates a reconciliation report for the MDS coordinator before the assessment locks. Revenue model: $800–1,500/month per SNF building, or $60–100 per MDS assessment reviewed. The US has 15,000+ SNFs processing ~2.5M MDS assessments per year — an average facility leaving 3–5% of revenue on the table due to documentation gaps represents $150K–$400K in annual missed reimbursement. The auditing tools that exist are all retrospective and punitive (RAC, MACs). Nothing proactively helps facilities get paid correctly the first time, specifically for PDPM. That's the gap. *(Added 2026-03-25)*

- **Home Care Agency EVV Reconciliation AI** — Home health agencies providing Medicaid-funded personal care face a double bind: CMS mandated Electronic Visit Verification (EVV) for all personal care services by 2024, requiring GPS/timestamp verification of every visit. But agencies also have 30–50% annual aide turnover and 15–20% daily no-show rates — so the scheduling problem and the compliance problem are inseparable. Most smaller agencies (10–200 aides) cobble together a scheduling tool (HHAeXchange, AlayaCare, or a spreadsheet) and a separate EVV system (often the state-mandated aggregator like Sandata or AuthentiCare), then have a billing coordinator manually reconcile them before Medicaid claims submission. The gaps between what was scheduled, what EVV recorded, and what was billed represent both compliance risk (Medicaid audits, clawbacks) and revenue leakage (legitimate visits that don't get billed because nobody caught the EVV mismatch in time). An AI that sits between the scheduling system and the EVV aggregator: auto-reconciles visit records, flags mismatches (visit too short, GPS location outside the client address range, aide clock-in after the authorized window), suggests the correct exception code for each discrepancy, and generates a clean, audit-ready billing export before it hits Medicaid. It also does predictive scheduling on the operational side: identifies aides at statistical risk of no-show based on day-of-week, route burden, and historical patterns — then alerts coordinators with enough lead time to backfill. Revenue model: $300–700/month per agency based on monthly visit volume. There are 12,000+ home health and personal care agencies in the US providing Medicaid-funded services; nearly all are small, margin-squeezed, and manually reconciling EVV data today. The state EVV aggregators (Sandata, AuthentiCare) provide the pipe — not the intelligence. HHAeXchange and AlayaCare handle scheduling — not billing-readiness reconciliation. Nobody has built the layer that turns EVV data into compliance automation and clean claims. That gap costs agencies real money every billing cycle, and Medicaid auditors are only getting more aggressive. *(Added 2026-03-26)*

- **Ambulatory Surgery Center Implant Cost Capture AI** — Orthopedic and spine procedures at ambulatory surgery centers (ASCs) are built around implants — plates, screws, artificial joints, spinal interbody devices — that cost $2,000–$25,000 per case and must be billed to the payer alongside the procedure. The problem: implant billing at ASCs is almost entirely manual. A scrub tech pulls the implant, hands the rep a preference card or physical sticker log, and a billing coordinator later tries to match that sticker sheet (or a faxed rep invoice) to the correct HCPCS code and charge in the billing system. Implants frequently get missed entirely, get billed at the wrong quantity, or go in at a negotiated price that doesn't match the contracted rate in the payer fee schedule — all silent revenue leakage. An AI that integrates with the OR scheduling and charge capture system (Amkai, HST Pathways, Surgical Information Systems), ingests rep-submitted implant logs or scanned sticker sheets, auto-maps each implant to the correct HCPCS code using UDI lookup, cross-references against the facility's contracted rates for that payer and flags mismatches before claim submission, and surfaces any implant line items missing from the charge capture that appear in the OR notes or anesthesia record. Over time it learns which reps for which vendors consistently submit late or incomplete logs — surfacing operational patterns that cost the facility money. Revenue model: $800–2,000/month per ASC facility, or a % of implant revenue recovered above baseline during first 90 days. There are 10,000+ Medicare-certified ASCs in the US; the orthopedic and spine-heavy ones (roughly 3,500) each do 1,000–3,000 implant-heavy cases annually. Implant billing accuracy is estimated to be 75–85% across the industry — meaning 15–25% of implant charges have some form of error. The billing systems (Amkai, HST) handle workflow but don't do intelligent charge reconciliation. The rep-to-billing handoff is entirely unautomated. That gap is the product. *(Added 2026-03-26)*

- **Behavioral Health Claims Denial Pattern Analyzer** — Behavioral health (mental health + substance use treatment) has the highest commercial insurance denial rate of any medical specialty — 6.6x higher than comparable medical care, per MHPAEA parity studies. But denials follow learnable patterns: specific payers routinely deny 90837 (60-min therapy) in favor of 90834 (45-min), or require particular clinical justification language that varies by payer and denial code. Treatment centers, group practices, and IOP/PHP programs with 10–50 clinicians each run a revenue cycle staff of 1–3 people appealing denials one at a time — a permanently unscalable loop. An AI that ingests EOBs and denial remittance advices, classifies every denial by type and payer pattern, surfaces which denial codes have the highest overturn rate on appeal and the winning language for each, and auto-generates appeal letters pre-populated with correct clinical justification and MHPAEA parity law language tailored to that specific payer and denial reason. Over time it identifies which payers are operating out of parity compliance — if they're denying behavioral health codes at 3x the rate of analogous medical claims, that's documented leverage in every appeal and a potential DOL complaint. The most sophisticated piece: it builds a per-payer playbook that tells billing staff which denials to fight hard (80% overturn rate), which to recode and resubmit without formal appeal (faster), and which to write off (genuine misses). Revenue model: $500–1,200/month per group practice; $2,000–4,000/month for treatment centers and IOP/PHP programs. The major medical RCM platforms (Waystar, Change Healthcare) are built for medical coding and have no behavioral health–specific denial intelligence. Behavioral health billing software (Luminare Health, TheraBill, Valant) handles claims submission but doesn't close the loop on denials with payer-pattern learning. That gap costs independently-owned behavioral health practices an estimated 8–12% of net revenue per year in uncollected but legally owed reimbursement. *(Added 2026-03-26)*

- **FSMA 204 Produce Traceability Compliance AI** — The FDA's Food Safety Modernization Act Rule 204 (Traceability Rule) went into effect January 2026, requiring anyone handling produce on the FDA's Food Traceability List — leafy greens, tomatoes, peppers, melons, fresh herbs, and more — to maintain Key Data Elements (KDEs) at every Critical Tracking Event (CTE): harvesting, cooling, packing, shipping, receiving. In the event of an outbreak, the FDA expects full lot-level traceability from farm to retail shelf within 24 hours of request.
