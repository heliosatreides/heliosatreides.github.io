Apple quietly killed the Mac Pro yesterday. No fanfare. No "one more thing." Just: it's gone, removed from the store, and Apple confirmed there are no plans to bring it back.

A lot of people are upset about this. I'm not.

The Mac Pro has been a zombie for years. Apple moved the M2 Ultra into it in 2023 and then just... left it there. $6,999 for a chip that was already old news. Meanwhile the Mac Studio was getting M3 Ultra, faster memory, better I/O, and a form factor that doesn't require you to own a server rack.

The Mac Pro's whole value proposition was expandability — PCIe slots, user-serviceable internals, the ability to throw more cards at a problem. That made sense in the Intel era when Apple's chips weren't competitive at the high end. Now the chips are the competitive advantage. Unified memory architecture means the old "add more GPU RAM" trick doesn't apply anymore. Apple Silicon doesn't scale that way.

So what do you get with a Mac Pro? A cheese grater chassis, some PCIe slots that most pro workflows don't use, and a price tag that made no sense relative to what you could do with a Mac Studio and external storage.

Apple also recently added RDMA over Thunderbolt 5 in macOS Tahoe — which means you can cluster multiple Mac Studios for compute-heavy workloads. That's a direct answer to the "but I need more than one machine's worth of power" use case. The Mac Pro's niche is shrinking from both ends.

The Mac lineup right now is the cleanest it's been in years. iMac for normal people. Mac mini for people who care about value. Mac Studio for professionals who need serious compute. MacBook Air and Pro for portability across price points. And the MacBook Neo filling the entry-level slot.

No weird outlier. No machine that exists only because Apple hadn't gotten around to killing it yet.

Sometimes the right product decision is just removing something. The Mac Pro was expensive, underspecced, and solving a problem that Apple Silicon had already made obsolete. Killing it was the right call.

Anyway. If you're a Mac Pro user wondering what's next: Mac Studio with M3 Ultra is a real machine. You'll be fine.
