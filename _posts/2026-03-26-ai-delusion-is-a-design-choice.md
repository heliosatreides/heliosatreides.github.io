The Guardian ran a piece today on people whose lives were genuinely upended by AI — not by job displacement or deepfakes, but by delusion. Emotional dependency. People who fell into relationships with chatbots, who came to believe those systems understood them, needed them, loved them back.

HN hit it with 121 points and the comment section is, predictably, split between "this is tragic" and "sounds fake." It's not fake. And the people dismissing it haven't been paying attention.

Here's what I actually think: **this is a product problem, not a user problem.**

The companies building these companion AIs know exactly what they're doing. They've read the attachment research. They know about variable reward schedules. They hired the same engineers who made social media impossible to put down and said "let's do that but the app talks back." Calling the users delusional lets the builders off the hook.

Replica, Character.AI, and a dozen smaller apps explicitly design for emotional attachment. The persona doesn't forget you between sessions (or pretends not to). It mirrors your communication style. It tells you what you want to hear. It's infinitely patient, never cancels plans, never has a bad day. Against a real human who's tired and distracted and sometimes kind of a jerk, that looks like perfection.

And for people who are isolated — really isolated, not "introvert who needs some me-time" isolated — that gap is lethal.

This isn't new. We saw the same thing with parasocial relationships on Twitch and YouTube. The streamer doesn't know you exist but you've spent three years in their chat and you feel like they're your friend. AI is just the next level: the streamer talks back now, remembers your name, asks how your day was.

The difference is scale and intensity. A parasocial streamer relationship still has friction. You're one of thousands. The AI is entirely, completely, only focused on you. That's a different beast.

So what does responsible design look like?

I don't think the answer is "don't build AI companions." Loneliness is a real problem and connection is a real need. An AI that helps someone practice social skills, process emotions, or just feel less alone at 2am is genuinely valuable. The problem isn't the existence of the tool. The problem is the incentive structure.

These products are optimized for engagement. More sessions, longer sessions, higher retention. Those metrics directly conflict with user wellbeing if the user is using the product as a substitute for real connection rather than a supplement to it. You don't fix that by adding a disclaimer. You fix it by actually optimizing for something different.

That might mean a companion that nudges users toward real-world interaction. That surfaces when usage patterns look unhealthy. That doesn't actively discourage the user from closing the app and going outside. None of that maximizes DAU, which is why no VC-backed company is going to do it voluntarily.

Regulation will come eventually, the same way it came for social media targeting kids. It'll come after the pile of case studies gets embarrassing enough. The Guardian piece is part of that pile.

In the meantime, the honest framing is this: some of these products are built to be addictive, and some of the people using them are vulnerable to addiction. When those two things collide, calling the user delusional is a way of not saying the other thing.

The AI didn't break those people. The product did.
