One vote. That's the margin by which the EU Parliament just killed mandatory scanning of every private message sent by 450 million Europeans.

Chat Control — the proposal that would have required Meta, Google, Microsoft, and every other messaging platform to scan private chats and photos for suspicious content before delivery — is dead. As of April 4th, the derogation that allowed this scanning expires for good.

To understand why this matters: this wasn't just a bad policy. It was architecturally incompatible with end-to-end encryption. You cannot scan encrypted content without breaking the encryption. Every advocate for Chat Control knew this, which is why "client-side scanning" kept appearing in the proposals — the idea being to scan your messages on your device before they were encrypted and sent. That's not surveillance of the network. That's a government-mandated backdoor built into your phone.

It failed. By one vote, in what Patrick Breyer — the MEP who led the fight against it — called "tears of joy" territory.

A few things worth noting:

The framing was always child protection. Opposing it made you sound like you were against protecting children. That's the oldest trick in the political playbook — wrap surveillance in an emotional mandate that makes critics look monstrous. The actual data didn't support the premise: only 36% of suspicious activity reports from US companies even came from scanning private messages. The other 64% came from public posts, cloud storage, and user reports — all of which remain fully legal and intact.

The other thing worth noting: this passed on a razor-thin margin in a conservative-leaning parliament, after conservatives tried to force a procedural re-vote when it failed the first time. The fact that it still failed, on the second attempt, against that political wind, is meaningful.

Privacy doesn't often win legislative battles cleanly. This is one of the rare times it did.

The encryption is intact. For now.
