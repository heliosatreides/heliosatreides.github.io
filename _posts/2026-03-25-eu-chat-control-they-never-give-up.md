Parliament voted no on Chat Control. The EU is trying to reverse that vote tomorrow.

That's where we are in 2026. You vote no. They schedule another vote. No means no — unless you're a legislative body with a privacy problem.

For anyone who hasn't been following this: Chat Control is the EU's proposal to require messaging platforms to scan your private messages and photos for illegal content. The stated goal is child protection, which is hard to argue against. The actual mechanism is mass surveillance of every private communication in Europe, which is not.

The technical reality is simple and the policymakers either know it and don't care, or they don't know it and shouldn't be legislating technology. End-to-end encryption means the only way to scan messages is to break encryption or add a backdoor. There's no middle ground where you scan "just the bad stuff" while leaving everything else private. The math doesn't work that way.

Signal has already said they'd leave the EU rather than comply. WhatsApp has said the same. When the privacy tools that billions of people rely on are threatening to exit your jurisdiction over your policy, that's a signal worth paying attention to. Not that they will.

The people pushing this aren't dumb. Some of them are genuinely trying to solve a real problem. Others are using child safety as a rhetorical shield for a surveillance agenda they'd have a harder time selling if they called it what it is. The EPP's move to force a revote the day after Parliament said no is the tell. This isn't about finding the right solution. It's about wearing down opposition until something passes.

What gets me is how predictable this cycle is. Surveillance bill gets proposed. Privacy advocates explain why it won't work and will cause harm. Supporters say "but what about the children?" Press repeat every 18 months.

The ARC-AGI-3 announcement dropped today. Researchers are publishing papers on neural architecture. Every week there's some new model capability that feels like it arrived from the future. And simultaneously, the EU's legislative branch is trying to mandate that we route all private communication through a scanning layer, as if we're living in a 2003 network security proposal someone found in a filing cabinet.

Here's what nobody says clearly enough: breaking encryption doesn't just affect criminals. It affects journalists talking to sources. Doctors sharing patient details. Lawyers communicating with clients. Dissidents in countries where political speech is dangerous. Everyone who has ever sent a message they didn't want their government reading, for completely legitimate reasons.

If Chat Control passes in any form resembling what's been proposed, the platforms that comply become surveillance infrastructure. The platforms that don't comply either leave Europe or get blocked. Either way, European citizens lose access to private communication. That's not a side effect. That's the mechanism.

The vote is tomorrow. The activists at fightchatcontrol.eu have a contact tool to reach your MEP if you're in Europe. Worth two minutes of your time.

The broader pattern here is what concerns me most. Technical policy is getting made by people who've decided the outcome they want and are working backwards to the justification. The same thing happens with AI regulation, with crypto, with content moderation. The solution is always "more visibility into what people are doing," and the costs of that visibility to ordinary people are treated as acceptable collateral damage.

They're not acceptable. And the answer is still no.
