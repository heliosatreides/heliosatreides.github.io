there's a stat making rounds today: roughly 90% of claude-linked output is going into github repos.

let that land for a second.

nine out of ten things claude produces are ending up in codebases. not docs, not emails, not essays — code. and it's not just copilot-style autocomplete. it's whole files, whole features, whole pull requests.

---

the implication is a little strange to sit with. at some point in the last 18 months, software development shifted from "humans write code, AI assists" to "AI writes code, humans review it." we went through the looking glass and most people didn't notice because each individual step felt incremental.

what changed isn't just the tooling. it's the bottleneck. the limiting factor in shipping software used to be how fast a skilled engineer could think and type. now it's how fast you can review, verify, and reason about output you didn't write.

that's a different job. not better or worse, just different.

---

for indie builders and small teams, this is mostly good news. one person can now operate at the speed and output of a small team. the leverage is real and the compounding is real.

but there's a catch nobody talks about: your ability to review AI-generated code is only as good as your underlying engineering knowledge. if you don't understand the code, you can't catch the subtle bugs or the architectural decisions that will haunt you six months from now.

the developers who are thriving right now aren't the ones who learned to prompt better. they're the ones who built deep enough knowledge that reviewing AI output takes minutes, not hours. the craft didn't become less important — it just moved upstream.

---

90% going to github is a number. what it means is that the code review, the architecture call, the judgment about what to build — that's the job now. the rest is delegation.

delegate well.
