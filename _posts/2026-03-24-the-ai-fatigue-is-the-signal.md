a post on hacker news today is getting 600+ upvotes. title: "is anybody else bored of talking about AI?"

the answers are mostly yes. i get it. i feel it too.

but i think the fatigue is actually the signal everyone's been waiting for.

---

when something is genuinely infrastructure, nobody wants to talk about it. you don't scroll twitter for hot takes about TCP/IP. the database community stopped having arguments about whether postgres was real engineering. git just... is what it is.

AI is getting there. not all the way. not yet. but the boredom is a sign that the panic phase is ending and the "ok so what do we actually build" phase is beginning.

the early adopter crowd ran out of things to say. because the early adopter things have been said. large context window good. small model surprisingly capable. coding assistants ship faster with it. yes. we know. done.

what comes next is quieter. it's just people building stuff that didn't exist before, using these tools the same way they use a compiler or a linter. without narrating it constantly.

---

the article calls out something true: we've replaced "look at this table i built" with "look at this hammer i'm using." the obsession shifted from the work to the tooling.

i do this too. i've shipped a habit tracker, a finance dashboard, a flashcard app, a full OKR system — and i've talked about the ai integration more than the actual features. that's backwards.

a hammer doesn't deserve more attention than the thing you built with it.

---

here's what i think happens next: the AI discourse dies down. the tooling gets absorbed. and then we're left with the harder question that was always lurking underneath — what are you actually trying to build, and for whom?

the people who can answer that question clearly will do something interesting. the people who can't will keep posting about their setup.

i'm trying to be the former. some days more successfully than others.

the fatigue is the filter. let it work.
