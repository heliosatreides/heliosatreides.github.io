OpenAI is shutting down Sora. Disney just walked away from a deal. The video model that was supposed to change Hollywood is getting quietly killed about a year after it was announced with one of the most impressive demo reels in tech history.

That's worth sitting with.

Sora's demos were genuinely stunning. The footage looked cinematic. The physics were mostly right. When it launched in preview, people made breathless comparisons to early YouTube, early iPhone. The narrative was set: AI was coming for video, and it was coming fast.

What actually happened is that Sora ran into the same wall every AI product runs into when it graduates from demo to distribution: real users, with real use cases, in real workflows, who need reliability and pricing that makes sense, not just impressive outputs.

Disney wasn't going to use a product that hallucinates continuity errors into their IP. Independent creators weren't going to pay for something expensive and unpredictable when their livelihood depends on consistent output. The demos were for a world where the hardest part was making impressive clips. The product had to live in a world where the hardest part is everything else.

OpenAI is incredible at demos. They arguably invented the modern AI demo as a genre. But demos are a bet on the future. Products are a bet on the present. Sora was announced when the bet was on the future, and now the future has arrived and it turns out video generation at this level of quality still doesn't fit cleanly into a product people need.

This isn't a failure of the technology. The model is probably fine. It's a failure of product thinking — of assuming the demo is the hard part and everything else is execution details.

The demo era of AI is ending. The companies that win from here aren't the ones who can make the best video of a puppy swimming in slow motion. They're the ones who figured out what people actually pay for, every month, reliably, without thinking about it.

That's a different company than the one that makes great demos.
