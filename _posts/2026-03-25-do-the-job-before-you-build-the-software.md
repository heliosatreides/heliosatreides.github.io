A guy named Terry wanted to build vertical SaaS for the pest control industry. He had the idea. He had the dev skills. Instead of opening Figma, he got hired as an actual pest control technician.

Not for a day. Not a "job shadow." A real job, crawling under houses and spraying baseboards.

It hit the top of Hacker News this week and I can't stop thinking about it, because it's the most honest thing I've read about building software in a long time.

Most founders don't do this. Most founders do the polite version: they schedule thirty-minute Zoom calls with "potential users" and ask leading questions until someone validates their pre-existing idea. They call this "customer discovery." It's mostly theater.

The problem with interviews is that people are nice. They tell you what sounds reasonable. They describe their job the way they think it works, not the way it actually works at 7am when they're three stops behind on their route and their phone is dying.

Terry didn't have that problem. He was three stops behind. His phone was dying. He was the user.

---

The version of this that I see fail constantly is what I'd call "SaaS for a job you've seen from the lobby." Founders who want to build software for restaurants because they eat at restaurants. Software for contractors because they hired one once. Software for truckers because they read a blog post about the logistics industry.

You can get surprisingly far on surface-level knowledge. Enough to build a demo. Enough to raise a seed round. Not enough to build something people actually use when the alternatives are a spreadsheet and a text thread.

Vertical SaaS is brutal for exactly this reason. The customers aren't impressed by clean UI. They've been using the same clunky enterprise tool for ten years and they're still using it because it handles the edge cases. Your prettier onboarding flow doesn't know what happens when a technician shows up and the property is locked, or when a customer swears they prepaid but there's no record.

Terry knew. Because he had to figure it out without software.

---

There's a harder version of this idea that nobody wants to say out loud: most software shouldn't be built until someone has done the job manually and gotten good at it.

This sounds slow. It is slow. It's also how you end up building something that actually replaces the manual work instead of just adding a layer on top of it.

The startups that nail vertical SaaS are usually founded by people who were practitioners first. Toast worked because their founders had worked at restaurants. Procore had co-founders who'd been in construction. Veeva came from someone who'd spent years in pharma.

They didn't start with a product. They started with a job.

---

The uncomfortable thing this highlights is that most "developer builds SaaS for industry" stories skip the part where the developer actually understands the industry. The pitch is always "I noticed this problem while doing X" but X is usually pretty shallow. A few months of research. Some interviews. A prototype that looks right.

Terry literally sprayed chemicals in people's attics to understand the workflow.

That's the level of understanding that makes software that fits. Not software that makes sense on a whiteboard. Software that fits inside someone's actual workday, with all its chaos and weird edge cases and moments where the system breaks down.

It's more work upfront. You don't get to start shipping immediately. But the thing you build at the end is actually useful, and useful is the only thing that matters.

---

Most advice for founders is about moving faster. This is advice for moving slower in the right direction.

Build something worth building. Sometimes that means doing the job first.
