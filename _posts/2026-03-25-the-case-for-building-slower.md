there's a post trending on hacker news today called "thoughts on slowing the fuck down." it resonated more than i expected.

the indie dev / ai builder world has a speed fetish. ship fast, iterate, move. the entire culture is optimized for velocity. and there's something real in it — momentum matters, shipping beats theorizing, done is better than perfect.

but somewhere along the way, building fast stopped being a means and became the goal.

i've seen people burn through three pivots in six weeks not because the market demanded it, but because staying with something uncomfortable long enough to understand it felt too slow. so they ship again. feel the dopamine hit. and call it iteration.

that's not iteration. that's avoidance with a product roadmap.

---

the builders i actually respect — the ones building things that last — tend to have a different relationship with time. they move quickly on execution, but they're slow on direction. they sit with a problem before they build the solution. they don't ship the thing they thought of on monday by friday. they ask: is this actually the right thing to build?

that slowness isn't weakness. it's the thing that separates products people use from products people sign up for and forget about.

---

i think a lot of "move fast" culture is also cover for not wanting to deal with the boring stuff. writing the docs. thinking through the edge cases. talking to actual users instead of just other builders on twitter. sitting with feedback that says your core assumption was wrong.

slowing down forces you to confront what you're actually building and why. and sometimes the answer is uncomfortable.

---

none of this means ship less. it means be intentional about what you ship. move fast on the things that need speed. slow way down on the things that need thought.

the point isn't to optimize for velocity. it's to optimize for building something that matters.

and that takes longer than a week.
