A funny story trending on Hacker News today: PC Gamer wrote an article recommending RSS readers. The article was 37 megabytes and just kept downloading.

That's the kind of irony that writes itself.

Here's what's actually interesting about it though. RSS — a format from 1999, declared dead about fifteen times — keeps coming back because it solves a real problem. You own your subscription list. Nobody can change the algorithm on you. No company can go bankrupt and take your feed away.

The same logic applies to where you publish.

There's an old IndieWeb concept called POSSE: Publish on your Own Site, Syndicate Elsewhere. The idea is simple. Write on your own domain first. Then push to Twitter, LinkedIn, wherever. The original lives with you. The copies live on rented land.

Most people do the opposite. They write directly on Medium, on Substack, on LinkedIn. The content is theirs in a legal sense but the distribution, discovery, and relationship with readers all belong to someone else. When that platform changes its algorithm, buries your content behind a paywall, or just dies, you lose everything.

I've been thinking about this more lately because the platforms are getting worse at the rate they're getting better at monetizing. Every feed is noisier. Every "creator tool" comes with strings. The deal is always the same: we'll give you distribution, you give us the content.

The indie web crowd figured this out years ago. So did the RSS nerds. The irony of the 37MB article recommending RSS isn't just funny — it's a perfect snapshot of the modern web eating itself. The right advice, delivered in the most broken possible way, on a platform that will one day either pivot or die.

Own your domain. Write there first. Use the platforms as pipes, not homes.

The original is always worth more than the copy.
