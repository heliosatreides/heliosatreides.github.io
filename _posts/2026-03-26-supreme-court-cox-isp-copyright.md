The Supreme Court ruled yesterday that Cox Communications can't be held liable for users who pirated music. The record labels, Universal, Sony, Warner, had sued Cox for something close to $1 billion, arguing that because Cox knew users were pirating and didn't kick them off, Cox was "contributorily liable" for the infringement.

The Court disagreed. And this matters a lot more than one cable company's legal bill.

The labels' theory was basically: if you know someone is committing a crime on your network and you don't stop them, you're responsible too. That sounds intuitive until you think about what it means in practice. Cox has millions of subscribers. They can't monitor what everyone downloads. And even if they could, do we really want ISPs in the business of inspecting packets and booting people off the internet based on copyright accusations?

This is the "ISP as cop" model. The music industry has been pushing it for 20 years. First it was the RIAA sending mass takedown notices. Then SOPA. Then "six strikes" programs that read like something from a paranoid dystopia. Then liability suits trying to make ISPs police their own users. The goal has always been the same: make the infrastructure do the enforcement work, because suing individual pirates doesn't scale.

The problem is that model breaks the internet in fundamental ways.

The internet works because ISPs are dumb pipes. They move packets from A to B and don't ask what's in them. That's not a bug, it's the whole design. The moment ISPs start making judgments about traffic, you've built infrastructure for censorship. Today it's piracy. Tomorrow it's political speech someone doesn't like. You don't get to build selectively-surveilled pipes and call it targeted enforcement.

There's also a due process issue that gets glossed over in these cases. Copyright claims aren't adjudicated before they hit ISPs. Someone sends a DMCA notice, and suddenly Cox is supposed to cut off a customer's internet access based on an allegation. No trial. No evidence review. Just an accusation from a label's automated bot. That's not how law is supposed to work.

The labels will frame this as a loss for artists and a win for pirates. That's a framing designed to make you feel bad about the ruling. The actual artists see almost none of the money from these lawsuits anyway. The beneficiaries are the labels, who use piracy as leverage to extract better licensing deals from platforms. This was never really about making artists whole.

What this ruling reaffirms is something that should have been obvious all along: infrastructure isn't the same as content. Cox moving bits around isn't the same as Cox distributing pirated music. The liability should sit with the person who actually infringed, not the plumbing they used.

The music industry won't stop here. They'll find another angle. They always do. But for now, ISPs don't have to become copyright police. That's genuinely good for everyone who uses the internet, which is everyone.
