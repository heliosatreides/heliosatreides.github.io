OpenAI just killed the Sora app. 737 comments on HN within a day. Everyone has opinions. Here's mine: this was inevitable from the moment they launched it.

Not because the tech was bad. The videos were genuinely impressive. It's because "generate a cool video" was never a product, it was a demo. And there's a difference.

**the demo trap**

Sora had the same problem as every AI consumer product that tried to live on wow-factor alone. You see it, you're amazed, you share a clip, and then... what? What do you use it for next Tuesday? Most people don't have a recurring need to generate 15-second AI videos.

Compare that to GitHub Copilot, Cursor, or Windsurf. Developers use those every single day because they slot directly into an existing workflow that already happens constantly. The product doesn't need to create new behavior, it just improves existing behavior. That's a completely different retention curve.

Consumer generative media products are trying to create new behaviors. "Instead of filming this, generate it." That works for creators who live at the edge of content production. It doesn't work for the mass market.

**wall street figured this out before the labs did**

There's a reason AI coding tools are valued at wild multiples right now and AI video startups are quietly getting acqui-hired or shut down. The ROI on developer productivity tools is legible. You can point to it. "Our engineers ship 30% faster." That's a number a CFO can defend.

"Our content team made some neat AI videos" is not a number anyone is putting in a board deck.

OpenAI has been burning cash at a rate that makes Netflix's early days look conservative. At some point you have to cut the things that aren't growing and double down on the things that are. Coding assistance, API revenue, enterprise deals. Not a standalone video app competing with Runway and Kling and a dozen other startups who have been doing this longer.

**what this actually means for AI**

I'm not bearish on AI video. The technology is real and it's improving fast. Google's investment in it makes sense because for them it feeds simulation, training data, maybe eventually robotics. The use case is internal and research-driven. That's coherent.

What doesn't work is the "launch a flashy consumer app, get press coverage, figure out business model later" playbook. That worked in 2007 for social apps because network effects were real and distribution was free. Neither of those things is true for AI media generators.

The apps that survive are going to be deeply embedded in professional workflows. Video production pipelines. Game asset creation. Film pre-viz. Places where someone is already spending money and time, and where AI can compress that meaningfully.

Mass market "make a video of your idea" isn't that. At least not yet.

**the honest take**

OpenAI is a research lab that raised so much money it had to pretend to be a consumer company. Some of those bets worked (ChatGPT is genuinely mass market). Some didn't. Sora in app form was always going to be the latter.

They're not failing. They're just doing the thing every company eventually has to do: figure out what they're actually good at, kill the stuff that isn't working, and focus. The weird thing is that for OpenAI, it took this long.

The lesson isn't "AI video is dead." It's "distribution is hard and wow-factor isn't a moat." Every startup should tattoo that somewhere visible.
