a New Mexico jury just handed Meta a $375 million verdict for knowingly harming children. the finding wasn't ambiguous. jurors agreed Meta made "false or misleading statements" and engaged in "unconscionable" trade practices that exploited the vulnerability of kids.

Meta's stock went up 5% after-hours.

i want you to sit with that for a second.

the market looked at a jury saying "you knowingly harmed children and concealed it" and responded: great news, actually. $375M against a $1.5 trillion company is what, a rounding error? less than one week of revenue. it costs more than that to run their data centers for a few days.

so the incentive structure is intact. harm kids, scale the product, pay the fine from petty cash, appeal it, keep going.

---

what bothers me isn't even Meta specifically. it's that we've built an industry that apparently can't be meaningfully punished by legal systems that move this slowly. by the time any verdict lands, the feature that caused the harm is three generations obsolete. the company has already pivoted, rebranded, or acquired whatever comes next. the fine is priced in.

the whistleblowers did their part. arturo béjar, the facebook safety researcher who left and told congress the company knew and didn't act. the unsealed documents. the attorneys general from 40+ states. seven weeks of trial. and the result is: stock up, appeal incoming.

---

here's what i think is actually broken: we're trying to solve a design problem with legal tools.

the features that harm kids aren't accidents. infinite scroll, algorithmic content ranked purely by engagement, removal of friction from content consumption. these are intentional product decisions that a teenager literally cannot opt out of, because their brain isn't done developing. they don't know they're being played. that's the whole point.

you can't fine your way out of a design. you have to change the design.

the real intervention would be something like: no engagement-optimized feeds for under-18 accounts, period. no algorithmic amplification. no infinite scroll. chronological timeline only. build the friction back in. you want to keep showing a 14-year-old content for six hours? that's the product decision under trial.

but that would hurt the numbers. so it won't happen without someone forcing it.

---

the stock going up is a signal. it means the market believes Meta will continue operating roughly as it does now. the verdict doesn't change the product, doesn't change the algorithm, doesn't change the incentive to maximize engagement regardless of harm. it's a cost of doing business.

that's not cynicism. that's the actual information embedded in a 5% after-hours gain.

the people who designed these systems knew what they were doing. that's what makes this different from tobacco companies claiming they didn't know. we have the internal documents. we have the researchers who left because they saw the harm data and watched it get deprioritized. there's no knowledge defense available.

so the question isn't whether Meta knew. the question is whether $375M is the number that changes behavior. and the market just told you: no.

---

i don't have a clean answer here. regulation that actually works would need to be prescriptive about design. that's harder to draft and easier to lobby against. class action suits will keep coming, but they move slow and the fines stay small relative to the business.

the optimistic read is that this verdict is the first domino. more state cases, the federal trial still pending with YouTube, public pressure that eventually makes advertisers nervous. maybe it adds up.

the pessimistic read is that the first thing the jury said was "we want the max per violation," and the stock still went up 5%.

i know which one i'm betting on right now.
