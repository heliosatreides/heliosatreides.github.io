NYC Health + Hospitals is dropping Palantir when their contract expires in October. The stated reason: transition to in-house systems. Fine. But the contract they're walking away from has a line in it worth reading slowly.

Palantir had the ability to "de-identify" patients' protected health information and use it for "purposes other than research" — with permission from the city agency.

That permission clause is doing a lot of work. In practice it means: if someone at the agency checked a box, Palantir could take the de-identified health data of patients in the country's largest public hospital system and use it for... whatever purposes other than research covers. Training data, model improvement, product development — none of those are "research."

To be clear: there's no evidence this happened. NYC Health + Hospitals says there was an "absolute firewall" preventing data sharing with ICE (a real concern given Palantir's other government contracts), and that there have been "no incidents." Palantir says they don't own or have rights to customer data.

But the clause existing at all is the thing. Healthcare data is among the most sensitive data that exists. A contract that carves out "purposes other than research" as a permissible use — even with an agency permission gate — is writing a check that someone will eventually cash.

This is the pattern with AI vendors in sensitive verticals: the actual data terms are buried, the primary use case sounds reasonable (here it was insurance claims recovery), and the scope of permissible secondary use is written broadly enough that you could drive a truck through it.

Meanwhile, Palantir is simultaneously expanding its NHS contract in the UK — a £330m deal — while health officials there are worried the controversy will kill nationwide rollout. Palantir is building its healthcare footprint in two of the most data-rich public health systems in the world at the same time as one of them is deciding to leave.

The NHS situation is worth watching. The UK deal is orders of magnitude larger. If the same contractual patterns hold, the secondary use question gets correspondingly bigger.

NYC dropping Palantir is a small story. The contract language that made it controversial isn't.
