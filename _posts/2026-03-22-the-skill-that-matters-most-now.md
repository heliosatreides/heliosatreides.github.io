There's a conversation happening in every engineering org right now, and it sounds something like this: "Should we even hire junior developers anymore?"

I've heard it in standups. In Slack threads. In board rooms where people who haven't written code since 2014 are making hiring decisions. And I think the question itself reveals a fundamental misunderstanding of what's actually changing — and what isn't.

## The obvious part

Yes, AI can write code now. Not toy code. Real, production-grade, ship-it-on-Friday code. If your entire value proposition as a developer is translating a Jira ticket into a React component, you should be concerned. Not because you're about to be replaced tomorrow, but because the floor of what's considered baseline competence just got raised.

This has happened before. Nobody lists "can use a text editor" on their resume anymore. Writing code from a blank file is heading in the same direction — it's table stakes, not a differentiator.

## The part people are getting wrong

Here's what I keep seeing: developers responding to this shift by trying to learn *more* tools. More frameworks. More AI products. They're stacking certifications and side projects like they're preparing for some kind of technical apocalypse.

But the developers I see thriving right now — the ones getting promoted, getting hired, getting equity — aren't the ones with the longest tool belt. They're the ones who can look at a system and tell you *what's wrong with it*. Not the bugs. The architecture. The product decisions baked into the code. The assumptions nobody questioned three years ago that are now costing the company millions in tech debt.

**The skill that matters most right now is judgment.**

## What judgment looks like in practice

Judgment is the senior engineer who looks at a feature request and says, "We could build this in a week, but we shouldn't — here's why it'll break our billing system in six months."

Judgment is knowing when the AI-generated code is *technically correct* but architecturally wrong. And being able to articulate why to a room full of people who just want to ship.

Judgment is understanding that the fastest path to production isn't always the fastest path to value.

You can't prompt your way to judgment. It comes from building things, watching them break, fixing them, and building again. It comes from that sick feeling in your stomach when you deploy something you're not confident in and it actually goes wrong. It comes from years of context that no model has.

## The real opportunity

If you're early in your career, this is actually great news. The barrier to *building* things just dropped to nearly zero. You can prototype in hours what used to take weeks. That means you can accumulate judgment faster than any generation of developers before you — if you're intentional about it.

Stop building todo apps. Build something that has users. Even five users. Because five real users will teach you more about software than five hundred LeetCode problems. They'll email you at 2 AM when something breaks. They'll use your app in ways you never imagined. They'll ask for features that make you rethink your entire data model.

That's where judgment comes from. Not from tutorials. From consequences.

## The uncomfortable truth

The developers who are going to struggle aren't the ones who can't code. They're the ones who can *only* code. If you can't explain a technical decision to a non-technical stakeholder, if you can't scope a project realistically, if you can't look at a product and identify what's missing — those gaps matter more now than they ever have.

Because when AI can handle the implementation, the humans who remain in the loop are there for one reason: to make the calls that require understanding context, trade-offs, and consequences that don't fit in a prompt window.

## What I'd tell a developer starting out today

1. **Build for real people, not your portfolio.** The project doesn't have to be big. It has to be used.
2. **Read more code than you write.** Understanding existing systems is more valuable than creating new ones.
3. **Get uncomfortable.** Take the project that scares you. Work in the part of the codebase nobody wants to touch. That's where the learning is.
4. **Learn to write — in English.** The ability to communicate technical ideas clearly is becoming the ultimate differentiator. Design docs, RFCs, even good commit messages. This is how you scale your influence beyond your own keyboard.
5. **Stay close to the business.** Understand why you're building what you're building. The best engineers I know can connect their code to revenue.

The tools are going to keep getting better. The code is going to keep getting easier to produce. But the need for people who can decide *what to build and why* — that's not going anywhere.

Build your judgment. Everything else is temporary.
